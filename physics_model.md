# Governing Equations: Brownian-Dominated Magnetic Nanoparticle Dynamics

## 1. Assumptions and Regime
This model describes the dynamics of magnetic nanoparticles suspended in a viscous fluid.
1.  **Brownian-Dominated**: The Néel relaxation time $\tau_N$ is much larger than the Brownian relaxation time $\tau_B$. $\tau_N \gg \tau_B$.
    *   **Implication**: The magnetic moment $\mathbf{m}$ is rigidly locked to the particle's easy axis. Internal spin flipping (Néel relaxation) is **excluded**.
2.  **Overdamped Dynamics**: The Reynolds number is very small ($Re \ll 1$). Inertial terms ($m \ddot{x}$, $I \dot{\omega}$) are negligible compared to viscous drag.
3.  **Rigid Dipole**: The particle behaves as a point dipole carried by a spherical rigid body.

## 2. Governing Equations

The motion of the $i$-th particle is described by limiting forms of the Langevin equation.

### (a) Translational Langevin Equation
Describes the evolution of the position vector $\mathbf{r}_i$. Balancing the viscous drag force against conservative and thermal forces:

$$ \gamma_t \frac{d\mathbf{r}_i}{dt} = \mathbf{F}_{dd,i} + \mathbf{F}_{thermal,i}(t) $$

*   $\mathbf{r}_i$: Position vector of the $i$-th particle.
*   $\gamma_t$: Translational drag coefficient (Stokes' Law). For a sphere of hydrodynamic diameter $d_h$ in fluid viscosity $\eta$:
    $$ \gamma_t = 3 \pi \eta d_h $$
*   $\mathbf{F}_{dd,i}$: Net magnetic dipole-dipole force acting on particle $i$ (see section d).
*   $\mathbf{F}_{thermal,i}(t)$: Stochastic thermal force satisfying the Fluctuation-Dissipation Theorem:
    $$ \langle \mathbf{F}_{thermal}(t) \rangle = 0, \quad \langle \mathbf{F}_{thermal}(t) \mathbf{F}_{thermal}(t')^T \rangle = 2 k_B T \gamma_t \delta(t-t') \mathbf{I} $$

### (b) Rotational Langevin Equation
Describes the evolution of the angular orientation. Since the moment is locked to the particle, rotating the particle rotates the moment. Balancing torques:

$$ \gamma_r \boldsymbol{\omega}_i = \boldsymbol{\tau}_{mag,i} + \boldsymbol{\tau}_{dd,i} + \boldsymbol{\tau}_{thermal,i}(t) $$

*Note: In the user's notation request, $\frac{d\omega}{dt}$ appeared. In the overdamped limit, the equation governs the angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega} \equiv \frac{d\boldsymbol{\phi}}{dt}$, not the angular acceleration.*

*   $\boldsymbol{\omega}_i$: Angular velocity vector of particle $i$.
*   $\gamma_r$: Rotational drag coefficient. For a sphere:
    $$ \gamma_r = \pi \eta d_h^3 $$
*   $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{mag,i}$: Torque due to the external magnetic field $\mathbf{H}_{ext}$.
    $$ \boldsymbol{\tau}_{mag,i} = \mu_0 (\mathbf{m}_i \times \mathbf{H}_{ext}) $$
*   $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{dd,i}$: Torque due to dipole-dipole interactions (the field from other particles).
*   $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{thermal,i}(t)$: Stochastic thermal torque satisfying:
    $$ \langle \boldsymbol{\tau}_{thermal}(t) \boldsymbol{\tau}_{thermal}(t')^T \rangle = 2 k_B T \gamma_r \delta(t-t') \mathbf{I} $$

The orientation unit vector $\mathbf{n}_i$ evolves kinematically as:
$$ \frac{d\mathbf{n}_i}{dt} = \boldsymbol{\omega}_i \times \mathbf{n}_i $$

### (c) Brownian Relaxation Time
The characteristic time scale for the randomization of the particle orientation due to thermal fluctuations.

$$ \tau_B = \frac{3 \eta V_h}{k_B T} $$

*   $\eta$: Fluid dynamic viscosity.
*   $V_h$: Hydrodynamic volume of the particle ($V_h = \pi d_h^3 / 6$).
*   $k_B$: Boltzmann constant.
*   $T$: Absolute temperature.
*   Using $\gamma_r = 6 \eta V_h$, we have $\tau_B = \gamma_r / (2 k_B T)$ (definition varies by factor of 2 or 3 depending on whether it is relaxation of moment or anisotropy axis, here standard Debye time is $\tau = 3\eta V / kT$).

### (d) Dipole-Dipole Interaction
The magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_{dd}$ (or more commonly $\mathbf{H}_{dip}$ in magnetism code) at position $\mathbf{r}$ generated by a dipole $\mathbf{m}$ located at the origin is:

$$ \mathbf{B}_{dd}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi r^3} \left[ 3 (\mathbf{m} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}) \hat{\mathbf{r}} - \mathbf{m} \right] $$

*   $\mu_0$: Vacuum permeability.
*   $r$: Magnitude of distance vector ($|\mathbf{r}|$).
*   $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$: Unit direction vector ($\mathbf{r}/r$).
*   The total field seen by particle $i$ is sum of external field and fields from all $j \neq i$.

## 3. Implementation Details
The simulation integrates these equations using the **Euler-Maruyama method**.
- **Input**: `input.txt` (Viscosity, Temperature, Field), `material_input.txt` (Ms, Diameter).
- **Core Loop**: `simulation.py` updates positions and orientations at each timestep `dt`.
- **Output**: Hysteresis loops (Magnetization vs Field) are recorded to calculate heating efficiency.
